Research Projects

Below are selected graduate research projects from my time at Emory University. Each project applies advanced statistical methods to real-world public health questions.

Bayesian Spatial Modeling of Opioid-Related Mortality in Georgia

May 2025 – Present    Advisor: Professor Lance Waller, Emory University
Bayesian Statistics Spatial Analysis R Public Health

The opioid crisis has had a devastating and uneven impact across communities in Georgia. This project maps opioid mortality disparities at the county level using Bayesian spatial models, identifying persistent high-risk geographic clusters that can inform targeted public health interventions.

We integrate sociodemographic factors — including poverty rate and racial composition — into multivariable spatial models. Model fit is compared using the Deviance Information Criterion (DIC), and results are reported as posterior mortality rates with 95% credible intervals. A simulation study evaluates how neighbor-weight matrices and spatial correlation strength affect precision and statistical power to detect true geographic disparities.

Key methods: Besag-York-Mollié (BYM) model, Intrinsic Conditional Autoregressive (ICAR) priors, Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) inference, choropleth mapping.

Multi-Pollutant Modeling of Air Pollution Effects on Gestational Complications

October 2024 – Present    Advisor: Professor Howard H. Chang, Emory University
Environmental Epidemiology Survival Analysis R Exposure Assessment

Air pollution exposure during pregnancy is a significant concern for maternal and fetal health. This project investigates the joint and individual effects of 12 ambient air pollutants on gestational complications using a large Georgia birth cohort (conceptions 2007–2013).

We employ logistic regression and discrete-time survival models to estimate trimester-specific risk, accounting for high-dimensional pollutant exposures alongside demographic and socioeconomic confounders. Spatiotemporal pollution estimates are linked to birth records to build a comprehensive, analysis-ready cohort. Results highlight critical exposure windows and key pollutant-outcome pairs, providing actionable guidance for environmental health policy.

Key methods: Weighted Quantile Sum (WQS) regression, Bayesian Kernel Machine Regression (BKMR), discrete-time hazard models, spatiotemporal data integration.
